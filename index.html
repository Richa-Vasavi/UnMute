Pattern analysis
8 patterns to know by name — tap to expand
Gaslighting
Made to doubt your own memory or sanity.
Manipulation
They deny events happened or contradict your recollection of facts. You stop trusting yourself. Journalling the exact words said — immediately after — is the most powerful counter. It creates an external memory they can't rewrite.
DARVO
You raise a concern. They become the victim.
Deflection
Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim & Offender. You confront something real; they attack your character, and you end up apologising. Counter: "I notice we've moved from what I raised to how I raised it."
Love-bombing
Overwhelming affection — then withdrawal.
Control cycle
Intensity that feels intoxicating but doesn't match the relationship's actual safety. Creates dependency. It cycles — the warmth is real. So is what follows it.
Intermittent reinforcement
You never know which version you'll get.
Addiction pattern
Random hot-and-cold creates anxious attachment — exactly like a slot machine. The unpredictability is neurologically similar to addiction. That's why you can't stop thinking about them. It is not love.
Triangulation
A third person brought in to create insecurity.
Control
Mentioning exes, comparing you to others, or using a third party to validate their position. Creates competition where there should be security.
Coercive control
Your freedom is quietly, slowly narrowed.
Abuse pattern
Isolating you from friends or family, monitoring behaviour, controlling finances — often framed as "caring." Legally recognised as abuse in many countries. It escalates.
Emotional drain
Every interaction leaves you exhausted.
Chronic stress
You walk on eggshells, monitor their mood, manage their emotions while yours are dismissed. You adapt yourself around their volatility. Exhausting by design — it keeps you focused on them, not yourself.
Moving the goalposts
You do what they asked. It's never enough.
Invalidation
Each time you meet a stated expectation, the expectation changes. Creates permanent inadequacy. The goal is not the goal — the goal is your constant striving.

Proven techniques
Grey rock method
Become uninteresting. No emotion to feed on.
When: you can't cut contact but need to protect your energy. Works because narcissistic behaviour feeds on your reaction — remove the reaction, remove the reward.
"Mm. Okay."
"I see."
"Sure, whatever works for you."
Broken record boundary
State it once. Repeat calmly. Never justify.
Each new reason becomes a new thing to argue with. Repeat the boundary identically when pushed. The calm repetition itself signals it's non-negotiable.
"I won't be discussing this."
"That doesn't work for me."
"I've said what I have to say."
DARVO counter
Name the flip. Hold your ground.
DARVO works because it's fast and catches you off guard. Naming the move disrupts it. You don't need to explain the term — just the observation.
"I notice we've moved from what I raised to how I raised it."
"We can talk about how I expressed it — but the issue still stands."
"I'm not going to apologise for raising something real."
Disengagement without escalation
Leave a conversation going nowhere.
State it once. Don't ask permission. Don't explain further. Actually leave. Staying to "finish" only deepens the spiral.
"I'm not able to have this conversation right now."
"I'm going to step away. We can come back to this."
Counter-gaslighting anchor
Hold your ground when your reality is being rewritten.
You don't need to convince them. You need to not be convinced by them. State what you know and stop arguing about it.
"I know what I heard."
"We may remember it differently. I'm clear on what happened for me."
"I'm not going to keep debating whether it happened."

🤕 Head & brain High urgency
Tension headachesBrain fogMemory lossMigraines
Chronic cortisol literally shrinks the hippocampus, impairing memory and learning. Gaslighting compounds this by attacking your trust in your own recollection. Tension headaches come from sustained muscle contraction during threat responses.

What helps: Journalling as an external memory. Physiotherapy for neck tension. Most cognitive symptoms reverse after the stressor is removed.

See a doctor if: sudden severe headache, vision changes, or worsening cognition over weeks.
😬 Jaw & neck Moderate
TMJ disorderBruxism (grinding)Neck stiffness
The jaw and neck are primary stress-storage zones. Nocturnal teeth grinding is strongly linked to hypervigilance — the body stays on guard during sleep and damages teeth in the process.

What helps: Dental night guard for bruxism. Physiotherapy for TMJ. Heat and progressive muscle relaxation.

See a doctor if: jaw pain limits opening/closing, or persistent clicking sounds.
💔 Chest & heart See a doctor
PalpitationsChest tightnessHigh blood pressure
Chronic stress keeps the sympathetic nervous system activated continuously. "Stress cardiomyopathy" (broken heart syndrome) is a real, documented medical condition — not a metaphor. Palpitations during anticipation of conflict are classic hypervigilance.

What helps: 4-7-8 breathing activates the parasympathetic response. Regular cardiovascular exercise lowers baseline cortisol.

See a doctor if: any chest pain, palpitations over a few minutes, or breathlessness — rule out cardiac causes first, always.
🫁 Digestive system High urgency
IBS symptomsNausea before conflictStomach knotsAppetite loss
The gut-brain axis responds directly to cortisol. Many people are treated for IBS for years without the emotional cause being identified. Prolonged stress alters the gut microbiome and can cause structural changes to the gut lining.

What helps: GI workup first. Trauma-informed therapy has strong evidence for stress-related IBS.

See a doctor if: blood in stool or significant unintentional weight loss.
💪 Muscles & body Moderate
Chronic fatigueWidespread painFlinching at sounds
Fibromyalgia has a documented association with trauma. The exaggerated startle response — flinching at sounds — is a recognised trauma symptom meaning the nervous system is permanently on high alert. Protective posturing becomes structural.

What helps: Gentle movement (yoga, swimming). Somatic therapy. EMDR has strong evidence for physical trauma symptoms.
🛡️ Immune system Track over time
Frequent illnessSlow healingAutoimmune flares
Elevated cortisol directly suppresses immune function. Prolonged stress can trigger or worsen autoimmune conditions — lupus, RA, psoriasis, thyroid disorders. The body begins attacking itself under sustained distress.

See a doctor if: 3+ infections in 6 months, abnormally slow wound healing, or suspected autoimmune symptoms.
⚡ Nervous system Seek support
Profound exhaustionDissociationEmotional numbnessFreeze response
After sustained high-alert states, the nervous system can enter a shutdown freeze response. Emotional numbness and feeling detached from yourself is dissociation — a recognised trauma response, not weakness or being "dramatic."

What helps: EMDR, somatic therapy, or trauma-focused CBT. Grounding helps in the moment but doesn't resolve the underlying dysregulation. Professional support is needed.
🏋️ Shoulders & back Moderate
Shoulder tensionUpper back painPostural collapse
Chronic protective posturing — hunching, shoulders raised, chest collapsed — is a documented trauma response. It creates muscle imbalances and nerve compression over time. This posture often persists even after safety, requiring intentional somatic work to release.

What helps: Physiotherapy and postural correction. Somatic therapy to release the protective pattern itself.

What to tell your doctor
🏥
What to say
You don't need to disclose everything. Try: "I've been under significant relational stress for [duration]. I'm experiencing [symptoms]. I'd like to rule out cortisol dysregulation, thyroid issues, and inflammation markers."
Guidance
🔬
Lab tests to request
Cortisol (AM serum), CRP (inflammation), TSH/T4 (thyroid), Vitamin D, B12, full blood count — often missed in routine checkups.
